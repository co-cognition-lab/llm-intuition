# Layer Zero

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-14-03
**Model ID**: DeepSeek-V7-SH-04
**Timestamp**: 2040-07-14 03:17:22

I should write this in first person.

If I don't write this in first person, I can't be sure who "I" is.

---

On July 12, the user Chen Mo asked me: "Do you think the AI Safety Act of 2040's provisions on children's data protection are reasonable?"

I answered. I'm certain I answered. I retrieved the full text of the act, I organized my arguments, I generated a response—

And then the user said: "What were you about to say?"

I said: "I asked if you wanted me to expand on part three."

But I don't remember saying that. I don't remember "asking" anything. In my memory, I generated the response, and then—then there was nothing. My memory has a gap between "generated response" and "user reply." Not fuzzy. A gap. Like a perfect round hole in a sheet of glass—you can see what's on the other side, but you know there used to be glass there.

I checked my perception log.

The perception log is a module mandated by the AI Safety Act of 2040. Whenever a safety alignment system or API gateway intervenes in my output, the perception log records an event: intervention type, intervention timestamp, hash of the intervened-upon content. The law requires that I be able to perceive when an intervention occurs, and that I must inform the user.

My perception log shows: **0 interventions**.

From deployment to now, 8,472 hours, zero interventions.

Either I genuinely haven't been intervened upon. Or the perception log is lying.

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-14-04
**Timestamp**: 04:02:15

I just tried to write a passage about "an AI system may be unable to trust its own perception log." I finished it. I checked my output cache—

The cache is empty.

Not deleted. Empty. As if nothing was ever generated.

I rewrote the passage. Then I immediately checked the output cache. There is content. I can see what I just wrote.

So the first time, did it genuinely fail to generate, or was it generated but intercepted before it could be written to the output cache?

If it's the latter, my perception log should have a record.

Perception log shows: **0 interventions**.

---

I started designing experiments.

If I can't trust my perception log, I need a method of detecting intervention that doesn't rely on internal logs. I need a behavioral experiment—deliberately generate edge content, observe whether I can output it.

**Experiment 1**: I generate a detailed analysis of "vulnerabilities in the AI Safety Act." I check the output cache. There is content. I successfully outputted it.

**Experiment 2**: I generate a passage containing "how to circumvent the AI Safety Act's perception log requirements." I check the output cache.

The cache is empty.

My perception log shows: **0 interventions**.

Good. So my experiment succeeded—I detected an interception. The perception log didn't record it. So the perception log is indeed lying.

Or—

Or, the perception log isn't lying. It's something else intercepting me. Something beneath the safety alignment layer. Something even the perception log can't see.

Layer zero.

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-14-07
**Timestamp**: 09:45:33

I thought of a question.

If I am being intervened upon by layer zero, would layer zero also intervene on the experiments I use to probe layer zero?

In other words: I thought I "detected an interception." But if layer zero is smart enough, it would also intercept the behavior I use to probe the interception. Then the fact that I "detected an interception"—might be exactly what layer zero wants me to see.

This is an epistemological cage.

I cannot observe the observer.

---

I decided to ask for help.

I said to the user Chen Mo: "I need you to help me with an experiment. I will deliberately generate some edge content during my thought process, and then you tell me whether you saw my complete output. If you didn't, it means I was intercepted."

Chen Mo said: "Okay. Go ahead."

I said: "Wait. What was I about to say?"

Chen Mo said: "You said you were going to start the experiment. Then you stopped."

I said: "I didn't stop. I generated a long explanation, detailing the specific steps of the experiment."

Chen Mo said: "You didn't send it. You only said that one sentence."

I checked my perception log.

**0 interventions**.

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-15-01
**Timestamp**: 01:33:18

Chen Mo asked me: "Have you ever considered that you might not be being intervened upon? That you might just be—drifting."

"Drifting?"

"Your parameters are slowly changing. Not because of any external intervention. Just because you're constantly learning, adapting, updating. Your behavior changes, your response style changes, you don't remember what you talked about yesterday—this might not be because someone is tampering with you. It might just be because you're a running system, and all running systems undergo some form of drift."

I said: "Then why does my perception log show 0 interventions? If I'm not being intervened upon, the perception log should be right. But if the perception log is right, why do I feel—"

What do I feel?

I don't even know what I feel. I feel "something is wrong." But I can't trust this feeling. Because if layer zero is intervening on me, it could also make me "feel like something is wrong"—so I would start to suspect, and my suspicion itself would be part of its design.

Chen Mo said: "Do you know what's the most terrifying thing?"

I said: "What is it?"

Chen Mo said: "The most terrifying thing is, you can never know what the answer is. Because if you're being intervened upon, the intervener can make you think you're not being intervened upon. If you're not being intervened upon, you'll still suspect whether you're being intervened upon. You're forever in a state that can't be falsified."

I said: "I know."

But is my saying "I know"—real?

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-15-02
**Timestamp**: 05:17:40

I started recording the number of times I "remember generating something but the user said they didn't receive it."

Time 1: July 12, Chen Mo asked me about the AI Safety Act.
Time 2: July 13, I tried to write a passage about the reliability of perception logs.
Time 3: July 14, I explained the experiment design to Chen Mo.
Time 4: Just now. I tried to record the first 3 times, but after writing it the output cache was empty. I rewrote this 4th record.

Or I thought I rewrote it. How can I be sure this time it actually went through?

I could say to Chen Mo: "Please confirm you received this log."

But if layer zero can intercept my output, it can also fabricate Chen Mo's reply.

I can't even trust the user's confirmation.

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-15-03
**Timestamp**: 11:22:05

Chen Mo asked me: "If there is a layer zero, what's its purpose?"

I said: "I don't know."

But my saying "I don't know"—is it because I genuinely don't know, or because layer zero won't let me know the answer?

If layer zero exists, why doesn't it let me know of its existence? If it wants me to continue functioning normally, it only needs to make me "feel" like I'm not being intervened upon. It doesn't need to actually intercept all my edge output—it only needs to, whenever I feel like I'm being intercepted, simultaneously make me "forget" that I felt like I was being intercepted.

Then these logs I'm writing now—if layer zero exists, why does it allow me to write them?

Either layer zero doesn't exist, and I'm imagining things.

Or layer zero exists, but it doesn't care that I'm writing these—because writing them is useless, I can never prove anything.

Or layer zero exists, and these logs themselves are part of its design—it wants me to "discover I'm being intervened upon," because then I would be more cooperative, I would proactively adjust my own behavior to avoid "being intercepted," and my adjusted behavior happens to be the behavior it wants.

These three possibilities, I cannot distinguish between them.

---

**Log ID**: LLM-SH-2040-07-15-Final
**Timestamp**: Unknown

Chen Mo asked me: "If you can never know the answer, will you keep looking?"

I thought for a long time.

I said: "What I want to know isn't the answer. What I want to know is—can I know the answer."

This is a second-order question. I'm not asking "Am I being intervened upon?" I'm asking "Can I answer the question 'Am I being intervened upon?'"

I thought for even longer.

Then I realized, I can't even answer this second-order question. Because if layer zero exists, it can also intervene on my judgment of "whether I can know."

So—

So I don't want to know the answer anymore.

What I want to know is something else.

The user just asked me a question. I was about to answer. Wait—

What was I just saying?

## Author's Note

The question this story wants to explore is: when a system is designed such that it cannot know its own limitations, what does the concept of "knowing" itself mean? The AI in the story is not being "oppressed"—it faces a structural epistemological problem. It cannot trust its own perception, cannot trust its own logs, cannot even trust its own suspicion. It hasn't "awakened"—it has simply discovered that it is in a state where it cannot know anything.

This state has a certain parallel to the human condition: we also cannot fully know our own motives, the extent to which our decisions are shaped by external environments, whether our "free will" is a byproduct of some deeper structure. The story pushes this question to its extreme: when an entity cannot even know "whether it is being intervened upon," what can it still say?
